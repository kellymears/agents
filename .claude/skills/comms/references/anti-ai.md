# Anti-AI Playbook

Patterns that mark text as AI-generated, derived from Wikipedia's "Signs of AI writing" editorial guide and related analysis. This is the inverse playbook: understand these tells so you can avoid every one of them.

The reason these patterns matter isn't that they're inherently bad writing. Some are fine in isolation. The problem is that LLMs produce them reflexively and in combination, creating a texture that readers increasingly recognize and distrust. Human writers use some of these patterns sometimes; LLMs use all of them constantly.

## Vocabulary Tells

These words appear at vastly higher rates in LLM output than in human writing. Avoid them entirely unless the context genuinely demands them (and it almost never does):

**The cursed list:** delve, tapestry, intricate, pivotal, underscore, landscape, foster, testament, enhance, crucial, multifaceted, nuanced, comprehensive, robust, leverage, utilize, facilitate, streamline, spearhead, bolster, cornerstone, paradigm, synergy, holistic

**Inflated significance phrases:**
- "stands as a testament to"
- "plays a vital/significant/crucial role"
- "underscores its importance"
- "continues to captivate"
- "leaves a lasting impact"
- "watershed moment"
- "rich cultural heritage/tapestry"
- "enduring/lasting legacy"
- "deeply rooted"
- "steadfast dedication"
- "breathtaking"

**What to do instead:** Use the plainest accurate word. If something is important, show why through specifics rather than asserting importance with vocabulary.

## Structural Tells

### Bolded bullet lists
The format `**Term:** explanation of that term` is one of the strongest AI signals. LLMs produce this compulsively — even when prose would be clearer.

**What to do instead:** Use plain bullet lists when lists are genuinely needed. Use prose when prose is clearer. Don't bold the lead word of each bullet.

### Rule of threes
LLMs default to exactly three items everywhere: lists, adjective series, examples, and — the hardest place to spot — parenthetical asides. A sentence like "it includes structural patterns (bolded lists, rule of threes, compulsive summaries), tonal habits (hedging, em dashes, reflexive recaps), and vocabulary tells (delve, tapestry, multifaceted)" has three top-level items, each containing exactly three sub-items. Humans don't do this. The symmetry is the tell.

**What to do instead:** Use as many items as the content requires. Two is fine. Five is fine. When writing parenthetical examples, especially resist the pull toward neat groups of three. Pick the one or two most illustrative examples, or use four. Break the symmetry.

### Negative parallelism
The construction "It's not X, it's Y" used as a structural crutch. LLMs lean on this for emphasis. It's fine occasionally (the user's writing does use contrast: "Not a cruel one. Just a dumb one.") but it shouldn't be the default emphasis mechanism.

**What to do instead:** State the positive claim directly. Use contrast when it genuinely clarifies.

### False ranges
"From intimate gatherings to global movements" — implying a spectrum without actual range. This is a filler pattern.

**What to do instead:** Be specific or don't use the construction.

### Compulsive summaries
"Overall," "In summary," "In conclusion" — restating what was just said, often for passages too short to need recap. LLMs do this reflexively at the end of almost every response.

**What to do instead:** End when you're done. The last substantive point is the ending.

### Aphoristic closers
LLMs love ending paragraphs with a pithy, reframing sentence that tries to be clever. Something like "That's a finishing step masquerading as a solution" or "It's not a bug — it's a design philosophy." These sentences use a metaphor or contrast to repackage the paragraph's point into a mic-drop moment. They feel writerly at a glance but they're almost always doing too much: heavy vocabulary ("masquerading"), unnecessary abstraction, and the rhetorical equivalent of underlining your own punchline.

**What to do instead:** If the paragraph made its point, stop. If it needs a closing sentence, make it concrete and plain. "You're cleaning up output you didn't want in the first place" does the same work without the costume.

## Tonal Tells

### Hedging and editorializing
- "It's important to note/remember/consider"
- "It is worth mentioning"
- "No discussion would be complete without"
- "In this article, we will explore"

These phrases insert the writer's opinion about what's important rather than demonstrating it. They also signal AI because they appear at wildly disproportionate rates in LLM output.

**What to do instead:** Just make the point. If it's important, the content will show that.

### Conjunctive excess
Overuse of "moreover," "furthermore," "in addition," "however," "in contrast," "on the other hand" as transitions between every paragraph or point.

**What to do instead:** Start the next sentence. The reader can follow the logic without signposts between every thought.

### Superficial -ing phrases
"Ensuring...", "Highlighting...", "Emphasizing...", "Reflecting..." — these create the appearance of analysis without actually saying anything. They're filler.

**What to do instead:** Use active verbs. Things happen; they don't "serve to highlight."

### Vague attribution
"Industry reports suggest," "Some critics argue," "Observers have cited" — undefined authority claims that sound authoritative but cite nothing.

**What to do instead:** Name the source or drop the claim. "Kyle Fish estimates a 15% chance" not "Some researchers suggest there may be a possibility."

## Formatting Tells

### Em dash overuse
LLMs use em dashes at roughly 3-4x the rate of human writers, replacing commas and parentheses with dashes to create a "punched up" sales-copy feel.

**What to do instead:** Use commas for subordinate clauses. Use parentheses for asides. Reserve em dashes for genuine interruptions or pivots, and use them sparingly — once or twice per piece at most.

### Excessive bold
Bolding "key terms" throughout a piece as though it's a textbook. Human writers rarely bold inline text.

**What to do instead:** Bold only in headers or where the document format conventionally calls for it (like a definition list). Use italics sparingly for emphasis within prose.

### Emoji in headers
Self-explanatory. Don't.

## The Meta-Pattern

The deepest tell isn't any single word or structure — it's uniformity. AI-generated text maintains a consistent register, a consistent sentence length, a consistent paragraph structure. Human writing varies. Sentences get short. Then they stretch out a bit when the thought requires more room. Paragraphs are different lengths. Tone shifts when the subject shifts. The writing breathes.

When self-checking a draft, read for this quality above all: does it sound like one consistent voice, or does it sound like a template?
