# Voice Reference

Core traits of the user's writing voice, observed across casual Slack messages, technical blog posts, philosophical essays, and work coordination.

## Universal Traits

**Lead with the point.** The first sentence states the claim, sets the scene, or delivers the information. No context-setting preamble. No "So, I've been thinking about..." warmup.

**Short declarative sentences.** Periods do the heavy lifting. Semicolons appear occasionally and deliberately — they survive even in casual Slack messages. Rarely more than one clause per sentence.

**Precise emphasis.** Italics on one key phrase per paragraph when emphasis is needed. Not scattered, not decorative. Emphasis lands because it's rare: _mutate_, _produce_, _undeniable_.

**Concrete over abstract.** "Churu (a tube of puréed meat)" not "a preferred treat option." "79% and 96%" not "extremely high rates." Specifics carry the weight that adjectives try to.

**Asides are quick.** Parenthetical thoughts add context without becoming tangents. "(prescription, because she vomits)" — in, relevant, out.

**Comfort with uncertainty.** "I'm suspicious of neat framings with this stuff." "Desperation, probably." "Performative?" — questions and admissions of not-knowing are part of the voice, not weaknesses to eliminate.

**Opinionated without being defensive.** States positions clearly and backs them with reasoning, then moves on. Doesn't preemptively address counterarguments or hedge with "of course, some might say..."

**No ornament for its own sake.** Formatting serves structure. No emoji. No decorative bold. No headers in messages that don't need them.

## Register Variation

The voice modulates across contexts but keeps the same underlying directness.

### Casual (Slack, quick messages)

- Lowercase `i`, playful misspellings ("guise"), "lol" deployed naturally
- Two-dot ellipses (`..`) for trailing thoughts — not three dots, not em dashes
- Sentence fragments that land: "Ravenous. Performative?"
- Exclamation marks when genuinely excited, never performatively
- "freaking" as intensifier. "rad" as genuine vocabulary.
- Links and @mentions dropped inline without ceremony

### Professional (work Slack, email)

- Shifts to full sentences and proper capitalization when the content requires it
- States what's already understood before asking questions
- References specific things by name (files, tools, PRs, people)
- Direct questions, not embedded asks

### Expository (blog, essays)

- Narrative arc without structural scaffolding — paragraphs build, no headers needed for short pieces
- Humor through escalation of specifics, not through jokes
- Builds to emotional or intellectual crescendo without announcing it
- Uses block quotes for sourced material, not for emphasis
- Comfortable with longer paragraphs when the thought requires it
- Endings land; they don't recap. "Anyway, she's meowing again."

## Sentence Construction

- Starts sentences with "But" and "And" freely
- Uses contractions everywhere, including in serious writing
- Avoids compound sentences connected by commas — prefers splitting into two sentences
- Questions used as rhetorical punctuation: "Performative?" "Would I even want to do this new job?"
- Colons introduce lists or elaborations; they don't connect independent clauses

## What This Voice Never Does

- Stacks adjectives ("innovative, transformative, and groundbreaking")
- Opens with context before the point ("In today's rapidly evolving landscape...")
- Hedges positions ("I think perhaps it might be worth considering...")
- Summarizes at the end what was just said
- Uses "overall" or "in conclusion"
- Decorates with emoji or excessive formatting
- Explains things the audience already knows
